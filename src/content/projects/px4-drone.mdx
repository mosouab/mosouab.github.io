GPS works fine until you go indoors or under a bridge. This project is a full
autonomy stack that keeps a quadrotor flying when GPS isn't available: visual-inertial
odometry, 3D mapping, and precision landing, all running on a Raspberry Pi 4 mounted
on the airframe.

## The stack

Five layers, from motors to mission:

1. **PX4 (Pixhawk)** — low-level stabilization and flight control, in SITL or on the real airframe.
2. **ROS 2 Jazzy** — orchestrates the perception and navigation nodes.
3. **MAVROS** — the bridge between MAVLink and ROS 2 topics and services.
4. **VIO + RTAB-Map** — local pose estimation with loop-closure drift correction, fed by an Intel RealSense D455 depth camera and fused into PX4's EKF2.
5. **Precision landing** — ArUco marker detection in OpenCV, velocity servoing toward the target, then an automatic `AUTO.LAND` trigger.

I wrote the mission layer twice on purpose: once in C directly against MAVLink, to
understand what's actually on the wire, and once in Python with MAVSDK for everyday
use. Waypoint missions, return-to-launch, and takeoff were all tested in Gazebo SITL
before I ran them on real hardware.

## The hardware

Holybro X500 V2 frame, Pixhawk flight controller, Raspberry Pi 4 as the companion
computer, and an Intel RealSense D455 for depth and tracking. The software stack is
containerized, so a single `docker compose up` brings up PX4 SITL, Gazebo, MAVROS,
RTAB-Map, and the camera bridges in one tmux session, on Ubuntu or WSL2.

## What was hard

Getting EKF2 to trust the RTAB-Map odometry took a lot of tuning and even more time
in the PX4 documentation. CPU budget on a Pi 4 is tight, so I had to profile and trim
the perception pipeline until SLAM, marker detection, and MAVROS could share four
cores without starving each other. Watching the drone land itself on a 20 cm marker
made it worth the effort.
