Unplanned downtime is expensive, and the standard fixes all have the same three
problems: cloud monitoring streams sensitive sensor data off-site, supervised models
need labeled failure examples that real factories don't have, and most off-the-shelf
tools only know one failure mode. EdgeSense is my attempt to fix all three at once.

## How it works

The core is a **USAD-style 1D-CNN autoencoder**: a shared encoder and two decoders
that play a minimax game during training. It learns what *healthy* looks like from a
couple of weeks of normal sensor data — no failure labels needed — then flags
anything that drifts outside that envelope. A small MLP head on the latent space
adds remaining-useful-life regression for components that degrade gradually.

Everything runs locally on an edge device next to the asset. One model architecture,
three outputs an operator can actually use:

1. **Anomaly score** per window of sensor data,
2. **Health score from 0 to 100%** — one number, readable at a glance,
3. **Remaining useful life** in cycles, where degradation is gradual.

## Validated on three very different machines

| Dataset | Failure profile | Result |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Metro do Porto compressors | Sudden air leaks | Recall 88%, F1 0.69 (AUC 0.91) |
| Hydraulic test rig | Multi-component faults | AUC up to 1.00 |
| NASA C-MAPSS turbofans | Gradual degradation (RUL) | RMSE ≈ 15 cycles, correlation 0.93 |

My favourite result isn't in the table: when auditing the top "false positives" on
the metro data, two of them turned out to be **real, undocumented air-leak events**
missing from the maintenance log. The model found failures the humans hadn't
recorded.

## The on-site recalibration trick

Training in the lab and deploying in the field never goes smoothly. EdgeSense uses
two weeks of on-site data to recalibrate its alert threshold to the specific asset —
which roughly **doubled F1** (0.37 → 0.69) on the metro compressors compared to
deploying the lab threshold blindly. Cheap to do, huge payoff.
