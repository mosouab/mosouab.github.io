Unplanned downtime is expensive, and the usual fixes all run into the same three
problems. Cloud monitoring streams sensitive sensor data off-site. Supervised models
need labeled failure examples that real factories don't have. And most off-the-shelf
tools only know one failure mode. EdgeSense is my attempt to deal with all three at
once.

## How it works

The core is a USAD-style 1D-CNN autoencoder: a shared encoder and two decoders that
play a minimax game during training. It learns what *healthy* looks like from a couple
of weeks of normal sensor data, with no failure labels needed, then flags anything
that drifts outside that envelope. A small MLP head on the latent space adds
remaining-useful-life regression for components that wear down gradually.

Everything runs locally on an edge device next to the machine. One architecture gives
three outputs an operator can actually use:

1. **Anomaly score** per window of sensor data.
2. **Health score from 0 to 100%** — one number you can read at a glance.
3. **Remaining useful life** in cycles, for components that degrade gradually.

## Validated on three very different machines

| Dataset | Failure profile | Result |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Metro do Porto compressors | Sudden air leaks | Recall 88%, F1 0.69 (AUC 0.91) |
| Hydraulic test rig | Multi-component faults | AUC up to 1.00 |
| NASA C-MAPSS turbofans | Gradual degradation (RUL) | RMSE ≈ 15 cycles, correlation 0.93 |

My favorite result isn't in the table. When I audited the top "false positives" on the
metro data, two of them turned out to be real, undocumented air-leak events that were
missing from the maintenance log. The model caught failures the humans hadn't recorded.

## The on-site recalibration trick

Training in the lab and deploying in the field never lines up cleanly. EdgeSense uses
two weeks of on-site data to recalibrate its alert threshold to the specific machine,
which roughly doubled F1 on the metro compressors (0.37 to 0.69) compared to deploying
the lab threshold as-is. It's cheap to do and made a big difference.
